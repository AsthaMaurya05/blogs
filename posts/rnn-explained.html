BLOG 03 · RECURRENT NEURAL NETWORKS

A network that remembers what it just saw is how a machine learns to handle time.

I sat through MIT 6.S191's Lecture 2 on sequence modeling, transcribed it screenshot by screenshot, and cross-checked everything against Stanford's CS230 RNN cheatsheet. This is what I wrote alongside all of that — what "recurrence" actually means, why one hidden vector carries an entire sentence's worth of context, and the part that took me longest to really sit with: why the exact same weights get reused at every single time step.

01 · The Problem That Started Everything

Predicting the future needs the past

MIT's lecture opens with a deceptively simple example, and it's worth sitting with because the entire rest of the lecture is really just formalizing this one intuition.

You're shown a ball in a 2D space and asked: where will it go next? With nothing else to go on, any answer is a guess — a random proposal about where it might travel. But give that same question a bit of history — a trail of the ball's last few positions — and suddenly the problem becomes almost trivial. Everyone in the room would point to roughly the same spot. Nothing about the physics changed. What changed is that you were given context resolved in time, and your brain used it.

The core idea behind sequence modeling A huge class of real problems have data that's resolved in time, or can be modeled as a sequence: audio waveforms split into chunks of sound, text split into words or characters, medical signals, financial time series, biological sequences, patterns of movement, weather. In every one of these, the prediction at a given moment should depend not just on the current input, but on what came before it.

So why not just reuse the feedforward networks from lecture one? Here's the direct way to try it: take your feedforward layer, and apply it to the input at time step 0, get an output. Apply the exact same layer to the input at time step 1, get another output. Do this again at time step 2, and so on. This actually works, in the sense that you get a prediction at every time step. But look closely at what's happening — the time steps never talk to each other. The computation at t=1 has no idea what happened at t=0. Each prediction is made in total isolation.

That's exactly the ball problem again. Treating each time step in isolation is the "no history" case — every prediction is a guess with no context. If the past genuinely carries information relevant to the present (and in sequential data, it almost always does), then a model that can't see the past is leaving that information on the table.

Concretely, what we need The predicted output at a later time step — say t=2 — should depend somehow on the inputs at the prior time steps, x₁ and x₀. We need a way to preserve information from the past and carry it forward to inform predictions later on. That single requirement is the entire motivation for recurrence.
02 · Building Up From What We Already Know

From perceptron, to layer, to something with memory

MIT builds this up in small, deliberate steps rather than dropping the RNN diagram on you directly. It's worth walking through each step, because the final architecture is just three small changes stacked on top of each other.

Step 1 — the perceptron you already know. Inputs x⁽¹⁾ … x⁽ᵐ⁾, each multiplied by a weight, summed, passed through a nonlinearity, producing one output ŷ. Nothing new here — this is the exact same unit from lecture one.

Step 2 — collapse this into a layer, at a single time step. Stack perceptrons together and you get a layer that maps a multi-dimensional input to a multi-dimensional output. Now label the input x of t — some particular time step t. But notice: this still isn't sequential. We're just looking at one snapshot in time. There's no link yet between this time step and any other.

Step 3 — apply that same layer across many time steps, but keep them isolated. Flip the diagram on its side, and imagine feeding in x₀, getting ŷ₀. Then x₁ in, ŷ₁ out. Then x₂, and so on. This is the "isolated time steps" version from Section 01 — and it's exactly where the problem lives. There is still no connection carrying information from t=0 into the computation at t=1.

Step 4 — the actual fix: link the time steps through an internal state. Introduce a variable h — the network's own internal state, maintained and updated by the network itself, passed from one time step to the next. Now the prediction at time t is a function of both the input at that time step and the state carried over from the previous one:

\[ \hat{y}_t = f(x_t, h_{t-1}) \] output — depends on — input, and past memory

That's the whole idea. Not a new network per time step — still one network — but its computation at each step is now linked to its own past computations through this state variable. This link, this looping-back of information, is what we call recurrence, and it's the reason this entire family of models is called Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs).

03 · Formalizing the Recurrence

One cell, drawn two equivalent ways

The RNN diagram gets drawn in two forms that confused me at first because they look so different — but they're the exact same computation.

the loop form
the unrolled form

FIG 3 — same computation, two drawings. Left: the loop shows h being fed back into the same cell. Right: "unrolling" that loop across time steps makes explicit that it's still one cell, called t+1 times, just laid out left to right for readability.

Formally, this is the recurrence relation — the rule that defines how the state update happens at every unit of time:

\[ h_t = f_W(x_t, h_{t-1}) \]
cell state function, weights W input old state
The detail that makes this work We use the same function and the exact same set of weights W at every time step. This is not a new network being created per word or per frame — it's one function, applied repeatedly, that happens to take a different pair of (input, previous state) each time it's called. This is why RNNs can process sequences of any length with a fixed number of parameters, and it's precisely analogous to parameter sharing in CNNs — same weights, reapplied everywhere, just across time instead of across space.

The pseudocode version

The lecture walks through this with a next-word-prediction example, and seeing it as code makes the recurrence click faster than any diagram:

my_rnn = RNN()
hidden_state = [0, 0, 0, 0]

sentence = ["I", "love", "recurrent", "neural"]

for word in sentence:
    prediction, hidden_state = my_rnn(word, hidden_state)

next_word_prediction = prediction
# >>> "networks!"

Notice the hidden state is initialized once, then threaded through the loop — each call both produces a prediction and updates the state that gets passed into the next call. By the time the loop reaches the last word, the hidden state has accumulated information from every word that came before it.

04 · The Actual Math

What f_W is actually computing

It looks intimidating with all the subscripts, but it's the same operation you already know from lecture one — weighted sum, then a nonlinearity — just applied to two things instead of one.

Two things happen at every time step. First, the hidden state is updated using both the previous state and the current input:

\[ h_t = \tanh\left(W_{hh}^T h_{t-1} + W_{xh}^T x_t\right) \]

Break that down: W_hh is a weight matrix applied to the previous hidden state, W_xh is a separate weight matrix applied to the current input. These get multiplied against their respective vectors, summed together, and passed through tanh — the same kind of nonlinear activation from lecture one, just hyperbolic tangent instead of sigmoid or ReLU here. That sum-then-nonlinearity pattern is exactly the perceptron operation from Section 02, just now taking two weighted inputs instead of one.

Second, the output prediction is produced by transforming that hidden state through one more weight matrix:

\[ \hat{y}_t = W_{hy}^T h_t \]
Three weight matrices, all learned W_xh (input → hidden), W_hh (hidden → hidden), and W_hy (hidden → output) are initialized randomly and updated through gradient descent — the same training process from lecture one. Nothing about these matrices is hand-designed; the network learns what information from the input and the past state is worth keeping, purely by minimizing the loss.

The Stanford CS230 cheatsheet writes this same relationship with slightly different notation — activation a instead of hidden state h, and separate activation functions g₁, g₂ for the two steps — but it's the identical structure:

\[ a^{\langle t \rangle} = g_1(W_{aa}\,a^{\langle t-1 \rangle} + W_{ax}\,x^{\langle t \rangle} + b_a) \quad\text{and}\quad y^{\langle t \rangle} = g_2(W_{ya}\,a^{\langle t \rangle} + b_y) \]

Different textbooks, different letters for the same three matrices and the same two-step update. Once you've seen it written one way, the other notation stops being confusing — it's worth getting used to both since papers switch between them constantly.

05 · Unrolling and the Loss

One forward pass, many losses, one sum

Because the network makes a prediction at every single time step, it also incurs a loss at every single time step. Training an RNN means adding all of those up.

\[ \mathcal{L} = \sum_{t=1}^{T_y} \mathcal{L}(\hat{y}^{\langle t \rangle}, y^{\langle t \rangle}) \]

This matters for how gradients flow during training too. Backpropagation on an unrolled RNN is often called backpropagation through time (BPTT) — at time step T, the gradient of the total loss with respect to a weight matrix W has to account for that weight's contribution at every earlier time step, not just the current one:

\[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}^{(T)}}{\partial W} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}^{(T)}}{\partial W}\bigg|_{(t)} \]

Since the same W is reused at every step, its gradient is a sum across all the time steps it participated in — one weight matrix, but its error signal is being accumulated from everywhere it was used.

06 · What You Can Actually Build With This

One to one is the exception. Sequences are everywhere else.

Once you have a network that can take an arbitrary-length sequence and carry state through it, four distinct problem shapes open up — and almost every interesting NLP or time-series task is one of these four.

ShapeTask typeExample
1 → 1Binary classification"Will I pass this class?" — student features in, pass/fail out
many → 1Sequence classificationSentiment classification — a whole tweet in, one emotion label out
1 → manySequence generationImage captioning — one image in, a sentence describing it out
many → manySequence transductionMachine translation — a sentence in one language in, a sentence in another out

The many-to-one case is the most direct extension of what we've already built: run the RNN across every word in the input, and only use the very last hidden state's output as the final prediction — by the last time step, that hidden state has accumulated context from the entire sequence.

One-to-many flips this: a single input (an image, say) sets the initial hidden state, and the network then generates output tokens one at a time, each one feeding back in as the "input" for the next step — this is exactly how caption generation and music generation work.

Many-to-many is the hardest and most general case, because the input length and output length don't even have to match — translating a five-word English sentence might need seven words in French. This mismatch is exactly why later architectures (encoder-decoder models, and eventually attention and transformers) exist: a plain RNN forces one output per input step, which doesn't hold once T_x ≠ T_y.

Where this connects to the rest of my roadmap This is also precisely the many-to-many, Tₓ ≠ Tᵧ case from the encoder-decoder transformer I'm training on English→French right now. The encoder reads the whole source sentence into a context vector (conceptually similar to a final RNN hidden state), and the decoder generates the target sentence one token at a time. RNNs are the intuitive predecessor to that architecture — transformers replace the recurrence with attention, but the underlying "sequence in, sequence out" problem shape is identical.
07 · The Catch

A plain RNN forgets faster than you'd expect

The recurrence relation solves the "no memory" problem in principle. In practice, vanilla RNNs are bad at remembering anything more than a handful of steps back, and the reason is a direct mechanical consequence of backpropagation through time.

Remember that the gradient of the total loss with respect to a weight has to flow backward through every time step it touched. Each one of those steps involves multiplying by the same matrix W_hh again. Multiply a value less than 1 by itself dozens of times, and it shrinks toward zero — that's a vanishing gradient. Multiply a value greater than 1 by itself dozens of times, and it blows up — an exploding gradient. Both happen because of this repeated multiplicative structure, and both get worse the longer the sequence is.

AdvantagesDrawbacks
Can process input of any lengthComputation is inherently slow (sequential, can't parallelize across time)
Model size doesn't grow with input lengthDifficulty accessing information from a long time ago
Computation accounts for historical informationCannot consider any future input for the current state
Weights are shared across timeSusceptible to vanishing / exploding gradients

In practice this means: a vanilla RNN can usually pick up on dependencies a few words back just fine. Ask it to connect a pronoun to a noun that appeared thirty words earlier in a paragraph, and the gradient signal carrying that dependency has been multiplied by W_hh thirty times over — by then it's typically vanished into numerical noise, and the network has effectively no way to learn that connection.

Gradient clipping — the partial fix for exploding gradients One practical technique: cap the gradient's norm at some threshold C before applying the update. If the gradient's magnitude exceeds C, rescale it down to C. This directly controls exploding gradients — it does nothing for vanishing gradients, though. The real fix for vanishing gradients is architectural, not a training trick, which is exactly why gated units like LSTM and GRU exist.
\[ \Gamma = \sigma(Wx^{\langle t \rangle} + Ua^{\langle t-1 \rangle} + b) \] the general form of a "gate" — a sigmoid-squashed value between 0 and 1 that controls how much information passes through
08 · The Fix — A Preview

GRU and LSTM: give the network a way to choose what to keep

This wasn't the focus of the lecture I just watched, but the Stanford cheatsheet lays out the fix cleanly enough that it's worth flagging here before the next lecture goes deeper on it — the fix for vanishing gradients is to add gates.

A gate is just a sigmoid-squashed value between 0 and 1, learned from the current input and the previous state, that controls how much of some signal is allowed through. Instead of forcing the hidden state to be entirely overwritten at every step (which is what a vanilla RNN does), gated architectures let the network learn to selectively keep old information or let new information in.

GateRoleUsed in
Update gate ΓᵤHow much should the past matter right now?GRU, LSTM
Relevance gate ΓᵣShould previous information be dropped?GRU, LSTM
Forget gate Γ_fShould the cell state be erased?LSTM
Output gate ΓₒHow much of the cell should be revealed?LSTM

LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) is, in this framing, a generalization of GRU — it has a separate cell state c⟨t⟩ in addition to the hidden state, and uses all four gates above to carefully control what gets written into that cell, what gets forgotten, and what gets exposed as output. This is the direct architectural answer to the vanishing gradient problem: the cell state has a much more direct, mostly-additive path across time steps, so gradients don't have to survive dozens of consecutive matrix multiplications to reach far back in the sequence.

09 · Two Ways To Make The Architecture Deeper

What if one direction, one layer, isn't enough?

The vanilla RNN only ever looks backward — at time t it knows everything before it, nothing after. And it only has one layer of recurrence. Two straightforward variants relax each of those constraints.

Bidirectional (BRNN)
Deep (DRNN)

FIG 5 — Bidirectional runs two RNNs over the same sequence, one forward and one backward, and combines both directions at each time step so predictions have access to future context too. Deep just stacks multiple recurrent layers on top of each other, the same "add more layers" idea from feedforward networks, applied here.

Where bidirectional matters vs. where it doesn't BRNNs only make sense when you have the entire sequence available up front — like classifying a complete sentence's sentiment, or tagging named entities in a finished document. They don't work for anything that has to generate output as it goes (like live speech transcription or next-word prediction while typing), because there's no "future" to look at yet.

If you forget everything else, keep these

  • Recurrence = one network, called repeatedly, threading a hidden state forward. Not a new network per time step.
  • Weights are shared across every time step. This is what lets an RNN handle sequences of any length without the parameter count growing.
  • The loss is summed across every time step, not just the last one — every prediction the model makes along the way contributes to the gradient.
  • Vanilla RNNs are bad at long-range dependencies. The gradient has to survive repeated multiplication by the same matrix across every intervening time step — this is why "what happened 30 words ago" is hard for a plain RNN but usually fine for an LSTM.
  • Bidirectional needs the whole sequence up front. Great for classification tasks on complete text, useless for anything generated live, token by token.
  • Many-to-many with Tₓ ≠ Tᵧ is the hard case — it's exactly what motivates encoder-decoder architectures, which is the natural next step after this.
