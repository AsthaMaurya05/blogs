BLOG 05 · ACTIVATION FUNCTIONS

Delete the nonlinearity from a neural network, and ten layers collapse into one.

In the neural-networks-from-scratch post, activation functions got a couple of paragraphs — sigmoid, ReLU, softmax, moved past quickly to get to backpropagation. They deserve their own post, because "which nonlinearity do I use" turns out to be one of the most consequential one-line decisions in a network's design, and the reasons why are more interesting than "it's just a squashing function."

01 · The Problem That Started Everything

Depth is worthless without it — here's the actual proof

"Nonlinear functions let networks learn curvy decision boundaries" gets said a lot, but it's worth actually working through why, because the reason is a clean piece of algebra, not a vague intuition.

Take two layers with no activation function in between — just raw weighted sums. Layer one computes z₁ = W₁x + b₁. Layer two takes that and computes z₂ = W₂z₁ + b₂. Substitute the first into the second:

\[ z_2 = W_2(W_1 x + b_1) + b_2 = (W_2 W_1)\,x + (W_2 b_1 + b_2) \]

Look at the right-hand side. W₂W₁ is just some other matrix — call it W'. W₂b₁ + b₂ is just some other bias — call it b'. The whole two-layer stack has collapsed into z₂ = W'x + b' — mathematically indistinguishable from a single layer. Stack ten layers, a hundred layers, it doesn't matter — without a nonlinearity between them, the entire network is secretly one linear function wearing a costume, no matter how deep it looks on a diagram.

What a nonlinearity actually buys you Insert a nonlinear function σ between the layers — z₂ = W₂σ(z₁) + b₂ — and that substitution trick breaks. There's no way to algebraically simplify this back down to one linear layer, because σ isn't a matrix multiply. This is the entire reason depth is useful at all: each nonlinearity gives the network a genuine new "bend" to work with, letting it carve out decision boundaries far more complicated than a single straight line or flat plane.

So every activation function in this post is doing the same fundamental job — introducing a bend that can't be undone by algebra — but the shape of that bend, and a handful of side effects that come along with it, differ enormously, and those differences are what actually decide which one to reach for.

02 · The Original Choice

Sigmoid — biologically poetic, mathematically expensive

Sigmoid was the default for decades because it maps naturally onto the story neural networks were sold with: a neuron that's off, on, or gently in between.

\[ \sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}} \]

Every large positive input gets squashed to something extremely close to 1. Every large negative input gets squashed to something extremely close to 0. That's a genuinely nice property for an output layer doing binary classification — you want a probability, and sigmoid hands you one directly.

The vanishing gradient problem, in one sentence Sigmoid's derivative, σ'(z) = σ(z)(1−σ(z)), tops out at exactly 0.25 (at z=0) and gets far smaller than that almost everywhere else, since the curve goes nearly flat for big positive or negative inputs. Backpropagation multiplies this derivative once per layer on the way backward — chain ten layers together and even the best-case 0.25 compounds to roughly 0.25¹⁰ ≈ 0.00000095. The gradient reaching early layers has effectively vanished, and those layers stop learning almost entirely.

This single mechanical fact — not fashion, not a change of taste — is why sigmoid was gradually pushed out of hidden layers as networks got deeper. It's also worth flagging one more issue in passing: sigmoid's output is never centered at zero (it's always positive), which turns out to slow down gradient descent in its own separate way, adding a slight zig-zag to the optimization path. Tanh, next, fixes that specific issue — but not the vanishing gradient one.

03 · The Zero-Centered Fix (That Wasn't Enough)

Tanh — same shape, one real improvement, same core flaw

Tanh looks almost identical to sigmoid on a graph, and it's tempting to assume it's just a cosmetic variant. One thing about it is a genuine, meaningful fix. One thing about it isn't fixed at all.

\[ \tanh(z) = \frac{e^{z}-e^{-z}}{e^{z}+e^{-z}} = 2\sigma(2z) - 1 \]

That last form is worth staring at: tanh is literally a rescaled, shifted sigmoid. Instead of squashing into (0, 1), it squashes into (−1, 1) — and that shift to being centered on zero is the real, practical improvement. A layer's outputs being centered around zero means the next layer's weight updates don't all get pushed in the same direction during gradient descent, which in practice noticeably speeds up training. This is exactly why tanh became the standard choice inside RNN cells (recall the hidden-state update from the RNN post — h_t = tanh(...) — this is precisely why tanh, not sigmoid, sits in that equation).

What tanh does NOT fix Its derivative, tanh'(z) = 1 − tanh(z)², still tops out at exactly 1 (at z=0) but still goes flat toward 0 at the extremes — the exact same vanishing gradient mechanism as sigmoid, just with a slightly better best-case multiplier. Stack enough layers of tanh and you still hit the same wall. Zero-centering was worth fixing, but it was never the whole problem.

This is really the pattern worth remembering across this entire post: sigmoid and tanh are both members of the same family — smooth, S-shaped, saturating at both ends — and that saturation is precisely what caps how deep a network built from either one can practically go, no matter which specific variant you pick within that family.

04 · The One That Made Deep Networks Actually Trainable

ReLU — almost embarrassingly simple, and that's the entire point

No exponentials. No curve. Just a hinge at zero. It's hard to overstate how much this one function unlocked.

\[ \text{ReLU}(z) = \max(0, z) \]

Feed ReLU a −7, you get 0. Feed it 12, you get 12 back, unchanged. That's the whole function — two straight-line pieces meeting at a hinge. Compare its derivative to sigmoid's: it's either exactly 0 (negative side) or exactly 1 (positive side), with no in-between and no shrinkage.

Why that fixes vanishing gradients A gradient signal passing backward through an active ReLU neuron (one that fired positive) gets multiplied by exactly 1. It comes out the other side exactly as strong as it went in — no compounding shrinkage, no matter how many layers deep the signal has to travel. That one property is a big part of why networks with dozens or hundreds of layers became trainable at all, and it's why "hidden layers → ReLU" is close to a default rule of thumb today.

It's also cheap. Comparing a number to zero is a fraction of the computational cost of evaluating an exponential — multiply that saving across every neuron, every layer, every training step across millions of iterations, and it adds up to real wall-clock time saved.

The trade-off: dying ReLU That flat zero on the negative side has a downside. If a neuron's weights end up pushing its input consistently negative — a bad initialization, or a large learning-rate step that overcorrects — its output becomes 0 and, crucially, its gradient also becomes exactly 0. Zero gradient means gradient descent has literally no signal to nudge that neuron's weights with — it's permanently stuck outputting zero, effectively "dead," for the rest of training. This is a real, commonly observed failure mode, not a theoretical edge case, and it's the direct motivation for the next section.
05 · Patching the One Real Flaw

Leaky ReLU, Parametric ReLU, ELU — give the negative side a pulse

Every fix in this section solves dying ReLU the same way: stop the negative side from being flat-zero, so there's always at least a small gradient to learn from.

\[ \text{LeakyReLU}(z) = \begin{cases} z & z > 0 \\ \alpha z & z \le 0 \end{cases} \quad \text{(typically } \alpha = 0.01\text{)} \]

Leaky ReLU replaces the flat zero with a shallow slope — usually 0.01 — on the negative side. A neuron that goes negative is no longer stone dead; it still carries a small gradient, small enough that it doesn't meaningfully change the function's behavior for positive inputs, but large enough that gradient descent can still nudge it back toward being useful.

Parametric ReLU (PReLU) takes this one step further and makes that slope α itself a learnable parameter, rather than a fixed 0.01 chosen by hand. The network gets to decide, per neuron or per layer, exactly how "leaky" it should be — a small amount of extra flexibility at the cost of a few extra parameters to train.

\[ \text{ELU}(z) = \begin{cases} z & z > 0 \\ \alpha(e^{z}-1) & z \le 0 \end{cases} \]

ELU (Exponential Linear Unit) takes a different approach to the same problem — instead of a straight leaky slope, the negative side curves smoothly toward −α rather than shooting off to negative infinity. This gives it a mean output closer to zero (similar spirit to why tanh beat sigmoid), which in some experiments speeds up convergence, at the cost of bringing back an exponential — some of ReLU's raw computational cheapness is traded away for smoother behavior.

VariantNegative sideExtra costFixes dying ReLU?

In practice, plain ReLU is still the most common default for hidden layers — dying ReLU is a real risk, but with sensible weight initialization (He initialization, specifically designed for ReLU networks) and a reasonable learning rate, it's manageable often enough that the simplicity and speed of plain ReLU wins out. The variants above are reached for when dying ReLU actually shows up as a diagnosed problem, not applied preemptively by default.

06 · What Transformers Actually Use

GELU and Swish — ReLU, but smoothed out

If you've looked inside a transformer encoder block — the MLP that follows self-attention in the ViT post — the activation function sitting inside it usually isn't plain ReLU. It's GELU.

\[ \text{GELU}(z) = z \cdot \Phi(z) \] z, multiplied by the standard normal cumulative distribution function evaluated at z

Where ReLU makes a hard, binary decision — a neuron either passes through completely or is zeroed out completely, based on a strict sign check — GELU makes that decision probabilistically smooth. Instead of a sharp corner at zero, it has a gentle curve: inputs near zero get partially passed through, weighted by how likely a standard normal variable would be to fall below that value. The practical effect: unlike ReLU, GELU is smooth and differentiable everywhere, including right at zero, and it even dips slightly negative for small negative inputs rather than clamping instantly to 0.

Swish — the closely related sibling Swish(z) = z · σ(z) — z multiplied by its own sigmoid. Discovered via automated search over candidate activation functions (Google, 2017) rather than hand-designed, and it turned out to consistently edge out ReLU on deep networks in their experiments. GELU and Swish are close enough in shape and behavior that in practice they're often used somewhat interchangeably — GELU became the more common choice specifically inside transformer architectures (used in BERT, GPT, and ViT), while Swish shows up more in some vision-CNN literature.

Why would a smoother version of "hard cutoff at zero" actually help? The working intuition: a completely flat zero region (like plain ReLU) creates a small discontinuity in the gradient at exactly z=0, and can contribute to the dying-neuron problem discussed earlier. A smooth curve has no such discontinuity anywhere, and empirically, these smoother activations have measurably helped very large, very deep transformer models train more reliably — enough that GELU has become close to a de facto default inside transformer MLP blocks, the same way ReLU became the default for standard CNN and feedforward hidden layers a decade earlier.

07 · The Output-Layer Special Case

Softmax — not really an activation function, more a translator

Every function so far applies independently to one neuron at a time — sigmoid doesn't need to know what any other neuron in the layer is doing. Softmax breaks that pattern entirely, and that's exactly why it lives only in the output layer.

\[ \text{softmax}(z)_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{j} e^{z_j}} \]

Feed softmax a whole vector of raw scores — say, 10 numbers, one per digit class, that could be anything from −50 to +50. It exponentiates every entry (which forces everything positive, and dramatically stretches out the largest score relative to the others), then divides each one by the sum of all of them. The result: 10 numbers that are all positive and sum to exactly 1 — a genuine probability distribution over the classes, with the largest raw score becoming the highest probability.

Why it has to see every neuron at once Sigmoid on neuron 3 only ever looks at neuron 3's own input. Softmax on neuron 3 needs the value of every other output neuron too, because the denominator is a sum across all of them — change any one raw score, and every neuron's softmax output shifts slightly, since they all have to keep summing to 1. This is a fundamentally different kind of operation, which is why it's reserved for the final layer's job of turning arbitrary scores into a genuine class distribution, rather than something you'd insert mid-network the way you would ReLU or GELU.

This is exactly the pairing referenced back in the neural-networks post's cheat sheet: softmax + multi-class classification goes with categorical cross-entropy loss, because cross-entropy is specifically designed to measure the gap between two probability distributions — and softmax's whole job is to hand it a real one to compare against.

08 · The Decision You Actually Have to Make

A practical decision guide, not just a list of formulas

Knowing eight formulas doesn't help much if you can't decide which one to reach for on Tuesday afternoon while defining a model. Here's the reasoning I'd actually apply.

SituationReach forWhy
Hidden layers, standard CNN or MLPReLUcheapest, fixes vanishing gradients, works by default
Hidden layers, ReLU is dying a lotLeaky ReLU or PReLUkeeps a small gradient alive on the negative side
Transformer MLP blocksGELU (or Swish)smoother gradients, the empirical standard in BERT/GPT/ViT

A pattern worth internalizing across all three architectures so far CNNs pair ReLU with hidden conv layers. RNNs pair tanh with the hidden-state update, precisely because it's zero-centered and the state gets reused every single time step. Transformers pair GELU with their MLP blocks. Same underlying problem — introduce a non-collapsible bend — three different architectural contexts nudging the choice toward three different answers, based on what property (cheap compute, zero-centering, gradient smoothness) matters most in that specific spot.

If you forget everything else, keep these

  • Without a nonlinearity, depth is fake. Any stack of purely linear layers algebraically collapses into one layer — this is provable, not just intuitive.
  • Vanishing gradients come from repeated multiplication of small derivatives. Sigmoid and tanh both saturate at the extremes, capping their derivative well below 1 — chain enough layers and the gradient shrinks toward zero.
  • ReLU's derivative is exactly 0 or exactly 1 — no shrinkage. That's the entire mechanical reason it unlocked genuinely deep networks.
  • Dying ReLU is real but manageable — good initialization and reasonable learning rates usually prevent it; Leaky ReLU/PReLU exist for when they don't.
  • Softmax isn't like the others — it's the only one that needs to see every neuron in the layer at once, which is exactly why it only ever appears in the output layer.
  • The "right" activation is architecture-dependent, not universal — ReLU for CNNs, tanh for RNN hidden states, GELU for transformer blocks, each for a specific, explainable reason.
